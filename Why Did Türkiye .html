Why Did Türkiye Down a Drone with an F-16? Weakness, or a Deliberate State Message?** The downing of an unmanned aerial vehicle (UAV) that entered Turkish airspace by an F-16 fighter jet of the Turkish Air Force has naturally sparked public debate. Why use an expensive fighter jet against a relatively cheap target? Was this a show of force, or a sign of vulnerability? These questions are not unreasonable. However, to arrive at the correct answer, the incident must be read not through everyday reflexes, but through state logic. --- Was This Incident a Simple Accident? Today, UAVs are not merely “flying machines.” States frequently deploy these platforms to: Test radar and air defense systems Measure reaction times Probe the question: “How far can we go?” For this reason, describing an airspace violation as a simple “accident” does not always reflect reality. Türkiye approached this incident not as a minor technical failure, but as a potential test. The key issue is not that the platform was unmanned, but the seriousness of the intent behind it. --- The 300-Kilometer Question: Vulnerability or Deliberate Monitoring? This is the point that has caused the greatest confusion. The UAV’s prolonged presence in Turkish airspace can be interpreted in two ways: First possibility: Türkiye may have chosen to track the target rather than immediately engage it, in order to assess its behavior and intent. This is a method commonly used in modern military doctrine. Second possibility: If this process is not properly explained, it can create the perception of delayed response. And this is where the real risk begins—because even a correct military decision can be perceived as weakness if it is poorly communicated. The issue, therefore, lies not in the intervention itself, but in how it is interpreted. --- Why an F-16? The “Expensive Force vs. Cheap Target” Debate States do not make military decisions solely based on cost calculations. Sometimes the central question is this: What kind of message is being sent? Using an F-16 conveys a clear message: “I control my airspace.” “I will use my highest capabilities if necessary.” “This area is not open to testing.” What was neutralized was not merely a UAV, but an attempt to probe boundaries. This was not a panic-driven response, but a calculated act of deterrence. --- The Black Sea and NATO Dimension: Who Was the Message For? This incident concerns more than Türkiye alone. The Black Sea has become not only a maritime domain, but also a zone of intensified air, radar, and electronic security activity. Türkiye is: A key country on NATO’s southern and eastern flanks A central stabilizing actor in the Black Sea region Accordingly, the message was not directed at a single actor, but at all regional observers: > “There is no vacuum here. The airspace is monitored continuously.” --- Why Did Türkiye Avoid a Detailed Public Explanation? This is a frequently asked—and valid—question. For states, silence does not always indicate absence; sometimes it is a deliberate choice. Türkiye’s limited public explanation can be understood for several reasons: The message had already been delivered on the ground. Detailed explanations risk triggering unnecessary technical debates. Excess detail provides operational data to potential adversaries. In such cases, the state’s position is simple: “The airspace was violated. A response was given.” The cause is not debated; the outcome is enforced. However, one point must be acknowledged honestly: While silence may signal strength at the inter-state level, at the public level it can create a perception gap. Such gaps are often filled by speculation—this is where narratives of “weakness” tend to emerge. --- Conclusion: Not Weakness, but a Threshold That Must Be Managed This incident does not demonstrate weakness on Türkiye’s part. On the contrary, it shows that Türkiye does not allow its airspace to be questioned and that it builds deterrence through action rather than rhetoric. Still, one reality remains clear: A strong state does not only act—it also ensures that its actions are correctly understood by both its own public and the international community. In the modern world, conflicts are fought not only on the ground, but also in the realm of perception. Military action and perception management are therefore inseparable elements of statecraft. States sometimes weaken themselves by speaking too much, and sometimes strengthen themselves by remaining silent. But there are moments when a single, well-executed action speaks louder than hundreds of words. The incident of 15 December 2025 was precisely such a moment. --- Melike Balkın Karakaya Senior Clinical Psychologist | Social Science Analyst Philosopher • Sufi Practitioner Analyzing Collective Mind Dynamics
