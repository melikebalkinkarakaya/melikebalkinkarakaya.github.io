Identifying the "Strongest Link": Who and How?

The crash of the Falcon 50 business jet carrying Libya's Chief of General Staff, Mohammed Ali al-Haddad, shortly after take-off from Ankara on the night of December 23, was presented to the public as a "technical accident." However, the ensuing debates focused less on the causes of the crash and more on how Türkiye would be positioned in the wake of this event.

For some circles, the answer was predetermined: Türkiye was the "weakest link."

This article stands precisely at this junction, posing several clear and critical questions:

  • Was Türkiye truly weak?
  • Was there a deficiency in terms of technology, security, or intelligence?
  • Or was the "weakest link" narrative more a product of political perception than reality?

Why Türkiye is Not Technically at Fault

First, a fundamental point must be clarified. This aircraft did not belong to Türkiye, was not a military platform, and was a civilian chartered flight. Civilian and chartered flights are connected to international civil aviation networks for maintenance, software updates, and avionic processes. These networks are not under the absolute control of a single state.

"The aircraft’s maintenance history and avionic software chain are not areas that Türkiye can control directly or unilaterally. This is not a vulnerability; it is the inherent nature of modern civil aviation."

Where Does the "Weakest Link" Narrative Originate?

In strategic analysis, the "weakest link" does not necessarily mean the powerless party. More often, it is the most visible and easily blamed actor through whom perceptions can be engineered. When the issue becomes politicized, Türkiye's real capacities are deliberately sidelined. This is not a technical analysis; it is a political reflex.

How Can This Event Appear to be an "Accident"?

Modern aircraft operate on digital avionic architectures. An electrical failure can affect multiple systems in a chain reaction, presenting the image of a classic "technical accident" from the outside. In these scenarios, no explosion occurs and no physical evidence of sabotage remains. Consequently, "electrical failure" is one of the most convenient scenarios to mask an intervention.

Defining the "Strongest Link"

The strongest link does not attack directly, takes no responsibility, and leaves no trace. Such an actor possesses cyber and electronic intervention capabilities, understands vulnerabilities in maintenance chains, and produces chaos indirectly. This is a capacity that not every state possesses.

Conclusion: Türkiye is Targetted, Not Weak

Presenting Türkiye as the "weakest link" is a narrative choice. Türkiye is visible and central; therefore, it becomes the first actor blamed in every crisis. The real picture shows that being the weakest link is often the mirror used to hide the power of others. The clues are hidden not in geographies, but in methods.
